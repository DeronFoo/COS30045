Contextual Background

Air pollution poses a critical threat in East and Southeast Asia where over 90% of the 2.5 billion population is exposed to unsafe air, resulting in approximately 900,000 premature deaths annually, primarily from fine particulate matter (PM2.5) linked to industrial emissions, fossil fuel combustion, and transboundary forest fires (UNEP, State of Global Air).

Urban centers like Jakarta, Bangkok, and Singapore face hazardous air quality exacerbated by seasonal and climatic factors, with Jakarta ranked the world's most polluted city in 2021, while smaller nations such as Brunei and Singapore suffer significantly from transboundary pollution (Science Direct).

This project visualises Southeast Asia's air pollution to illuminate its challenges, health impacts, and sources, driving awareness and fostering action toward cleaner air and healthier communities.

Aim & Objectives

This project aims to raise awareness of air pollution's pervasive impact on health, livelihoods, and the environment in Southeast Asia, inspiring action to combat this pressing issue. Through compelling data-driven storytelling, it seeks to empower individuals, communities, and policymakers to drive meaningful change for cleaner air and healthier futures.

  • Raise Awareness - Educate audiences about the scale and severity of air pollution and its far-reaching effects on health and the environment.
  • Foster Understanding - Demonstrate the link between pollution levels and their devastating impacts on public health to urge for intervention.
  • Promote Accountability - Highlight the sources and contributors of air pollution to advocate for responsible practices and policies.
  • Encourage Action - Motivate individuals, organisations, and governments to adopt sustainable solutions for improving air quality.
  • Inspire Collaboration - Encourage regional and international cooperation to address shared challenges and achieve long-term solutions.

The AQI data reveals a strong correlation between industrialisation, seasonal pollution events, and health impacts across different regions.

Key Takeaways:

  • Industrialisation and Traffic Pollution: Countries with high AQI values like India (487), China (211), and Germany (341) are major industrial hubs. In India, rapid urbanisation and heavy reliance on coal-fired power plants contribute significantly to air pollution; For instance, in cities like New Delhi and Mumbai, uncontrolled year-round vehicles and construction activities hugely worsen air quality. Similarly, China's industrialisation particularly in regions like Beijing and Shanghai, is a key factor behind its high pollution levels. In these countries, PM2.5 levels often exceed safety limits, contributing to widespread chronic respiratory diseases and increased mortality rates.
  • Seasonal Pollution Peaks: Regions like Southeast Asia experience sharp seasonal spikes in AQI especially in countries like Vietnam (83) and Myanmar (69), but Thailand (101) and Indonesia (134) are particularly impacted by hazardous air quality during specific periods. In Indonesia, agricultural burning including illegal land-clearing fires, contributes to the worst pollution episodes during the dry season when forest and peatland fires reach their peak; This causes AQI levels to soar, especially in Sumatra and Kalimantan, leading to toxic haze that spreads to neighbouring countries notably Thailand and Malaysia. In Thailand, the situation is exacerbated by local rice-field burning which usually peaks in March and April. Both countries face severe health consequences during these periods, with increased respiratory issues, cardiovascular diseases, and hospital admissions. The transboundary smoke particularly from Indonesia's fires, significantly worsens air quality in surrounding regions, including reaching Philippinesâ€”across Visayas and Mindanao, causing AQI levels to frequently exceed "unhealthy" thresholds.
  • Health Impact Correlation: Countries in Africa, such as Uganda (175) and Chad (165), suffer from high AQI levels due to a combination of urban pollution and natural factors like dust storms and biomass burning. In Uganda, the major cities like Kampala face poor air quality which leads to higher rates of asthma and other chronic respiratory diseases. In Egypt (161), where air pollution is aggravated by traffic and industrial emissions, there is a direct link to increased mortality rates due to heart disease and stroke. Long-term exposure to PM2.5 is recognised as a key contributor to premature deaths in these regions.

What is AQI?

The AQI represents air quality based on measurements of particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10), ozone (O3), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), sulfur dioxide (SO2), and carbon monoxide (CO).

Key Takeaways:

  • High Pollution = Health Burden: Myanmar and Vietnam are experiencing rising DALYs due to air pollution, despite some reductions in PM2.5 exposure. Urbanisation and industrialisation are key drivers of these trends, especially in cities like Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City, where heavy traffic and industrial emissions dominate. Visualisation Insights

The data provides a comprehensive look at the burden of disease by risk factor in Southeast Asia, as well as the impact of air pollution on various diseases across selected countries. These insights reveal significant patterns in public health challenges and improvements over the last decade.

Key Takeaways:

  • Air Pollution - A Persistent and Evolving Risk: Combined outdoor and indoor air pollution remains the top risk factor for disease burden in Southeast Asia, although it saw a modest decline from 91.21 (2011) to 84.32 (2021). Countries like Indonesia show a high burden attributable to air pollution for diseases like COPD (16%) and ischemic heart disease (16%), highlighting the need for improved air quality regulations. Indoor air pollution dropped significantly from 67.11 to 46.79, suggesting progress in cleaner cooking solutions in rural areas.

  • Rise in Chronic Diseases Linked to Modern Lifestyles: Conditions such as high blood pressure (from 45.47 to 58.68), high blood sugar (from 27.17 to 40.40), and obesity (from 13.25 to 20.92) are on the rise, reflecting growing health challenges related to lifestyle and diet shifts in urbanizing regions. In Singapore, air pollution contributes to diabetes (16%), highlighting the metabolic impact of urban environmental factors. Dietary risks, including a diet low in fruits and vegetables and high in salt, are also increasing, pointing to changing food habits across the region.

  • Reductions in Traditional Risks Reflect Public Health Improvements: Risks like unsafe water sources (from 25.55 to 14.01), unsafe sanitation (from 20.32 to 9.58), and child wasting (from 25.65 to 8.57) saw dramatic declines, likely due to enhanced access to clean water, improved hygiene practices, and nutrition programs. The nearly negligible burden of Vitamin A deficiency (from 1.97 to 0.55) and zinc deficiency (from 0.14 to 0.03) is a testament to successful micronutrient interventions in the region. Countries like Brunei showcase low attribution of air pollution across all disease categories, emphasizing effective policy implementation.

Visualisation Insights

The data reveals the significant toll air pollution takes on human lives, both globally and within Southeast Asia, highlighting it as one of the most critical public health challenges of our time. Below are key takeaways based on the analysis:

Key Takeaways:

  • Global Perspective: Air pollution ranked as the second leading cause of death globally in 2021, responsible for an estimated 8.08 million deaths. It is surpassed only by high blood pressure (10.85 million deaths) and is deadlier than smoking (6.18 million deaths) and high blood sugar (5.29 million deaths). Outdoor particulate matter pollution alone caused 4.72 million deaths, emphasizing the urgent need for stricter air quality regulations worldwide.Indoor air pollution, while declining in many regions due to cleaner cooking technologies, still caused 3.11 million deaths globally.
  • The Heavy Burden of PM2.5 in Southeast Asia Indonesia bears the highest burden of deaths caused by PM2.5 exposure in Southeast Asia, with 129,300 deaths in 2021. This reflects the severe pollution levels in densely populated and industrialized areas. Countries like Thailand (53,400 deaths), Philippines (43,200 deaths), and Vietnam (41,500 deaths) also show alarmingly high death counts, driven by urban pollution, deforestation, and reliance on coal and biomass energy. Smaller nations such as Cambodia (3,460 deaths) and Laos (2,360 deaths) experience fewer absolute deaths but likely higher per capita impacts, considering their smaller populations.
  • The Silent Killer: Air pollution contributes to a wide range of fatal diseases, including cardiovascular conditions, respiratory illnesses, lung cancer, and diabetes. Its non-communicable disease burden rivals traditional health risks like poor diets and tobacco use, linking directly to conditions like ischemic heart disease and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD). For instance, in Indonesia, air pollution accounts for a significant portion of deaths caused by COPD (16%), ischemic heart disease (16%), and lung cancer (12%), underscoring its multifaceted health impact.

Visualisation Insights

The data highlights the diverse and significant sources of air pollution in Southeast Asia and their contribution to regional health risks. Below are key insights drawn from the analysis, with actionable recommendations for policy interventions and best practices.

Key Takeaways:

  • Residential and Energy Sectors are Major Polluters: The residential and energy sectors are the largest contributors to air pollution, with residential sources accounting for up to 37% of pollution in countries like Vietnam. In countries like Indonesia and Brunei, the energy sector also plays a significant role. Addressing household emissions from cooking and heating, alongside cleaner energy solutions, is essential to reduce overall pollution.
  • Transport Sector Requires Urgent Reform: The transport sector contributes significantly to air pollution in urbanized countries such as Thailand (16.9%) and Singapore (18.5%). Transitioning to electric vehicles (EVs) and improving public transport systems can reduce emissions from road transport, which is a major urban health concern.
  • Windblown Dust and Fires Pose Regional Challenges: Windblown dust and landscape fires are particularly prevalent in Indonesia and Laos, contributing to seasonal pollution spikes. Regional collaboration on fire management and addressing slash-and-burn farming practices are critical to tackling this issue.
  • Agricultural Practices Contribute Significantly: Agricultural emissions, including the burning of crop residues, represent a major pollution source, especially in Indonesia (6.6%) and Thailand (4.3%). Sustainable farming techniques and zero-burning policies should be promoted to curb this pollution source.
  • Cross-Sectoral Policy Coordination is Crucial: Addressing air pollution requires policy coordination across sectors: energy, transport, industry, and agriculture. Governments must adopt integrated solutions that involve cleaner energy, transport reform, sustainable agriculture, and stronger regulations to reduce emissions across the board.
